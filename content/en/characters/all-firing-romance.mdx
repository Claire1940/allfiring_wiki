<Section icon={Swords} title="Revolutionizing Romance: Fire Emblem Genealogy of the Holy War (FE4)">
*Fire Emblem: Genealogy of the Holy War* (FE4) marked a monumental shift in the series' approach to romance, transforming it from a narrative backdrop into a core mechanical system. The game introduced a multi-generational structure where player-controlled pairings in the first generation directly influenced the stats, skills, and item inheritance of their children in the second generation.

<Callout type="warning" title="Strategic Pairing is Key">
In FE4, suboptimal pairings in Generation 1 can significantly weaken your Generation 2 units. Careful planning is essential to ensure your child units are powerful enough to tackle the game's challenges.
</Callout>

The "lover system" in FE4 was a rudimentary support system. Characters gained "lover points" by ending turns next to each other, with certain events boosting specific pairings. If a first-generation woman remained unmarried or died, replacement units (often weaker) would join the army instead of her children. This system encouraged players to actively manage relationships, making "all firing romance" a central strategic consideration.

<Section icon={Target} title="Support Systems & Paired Endings: GBA Era & Tellius (FE6-FE10)">
Following FE4, the Fire Emblem series refined its romance mechanics with the introduction of the modern support system in *Fire Emblem: The Binding Blade* (FE6). This allowed for more fleshed-out character interactions and the potential for "paired endings," where characters who achieved a high support rank would receive a special epilogue together.

<Callout type="success" title="Pre-established Relationships">
*Fire Emblem: The Blazing Blade* (FE7) uniquely featured Pent and Louise, a married couple with an A-support from the start. This not only provided immediate in-game bonuses but also showcased a pre-existing, strong relationship, a rarity in the series.
</Callout>

While FE6 and FE7 offered numerous potential romantic pairings, their canonicity was often ambiguous due to their prequel/sequel relationship. *Fire Emblem: The Sacred Stones* (FE8) provided a few more canonical suggestions, like Colm and Neimi, who start with a pre-established connection and no other romantic paired endings. The Tellius duology (*Path of Radiance* and *Radiant Dawn*) significantly reduced the number of romantic paired endings, focusing more on platonic bonds and established couples like Khalil and Largo. However, it also introduced more complex and sometimes tragic romantic subplots, such as Aimee and Reyson, and the controversial pairing of Astrid and Makalov.

<Section icon={Sparkles} title="Modern Romance: Dating Sim Elements & Avatars (FE13-FE17)">
The shift to the 3DS and later the Switch brought a dramatic transformation to "all firing romance" in Fire Emblem, largely due to the introduction of player avatars and expanded dating sim mechanics.

*Fire Emblem Awakening* (FE13) fully embraced the multi-generational model of FE4, but with a unique twist: children traveled back from a doomed future. Players could pair almost any character, including the avatar, leading to a vast array of child units with inherited traits. While there were no fixed canonical pairings, the game's code did have "preferred partners," influencing support tiebreakers.

*Fire Emblem Fates* (FE14) continued this trend, refining the support system and introducing pre-established romantic histories (like Kagero and Saizo). Crucially, Fates also marked the series' first foray into explicit gay relationships for the player avatar, albeit with limitations (one character per route) and mechanical disadvantages (no child unit Kana).

*Fire Emblem: Three Houses* (FE16) pushed the envelope further, doubling down on dating sim aspects with gifts, tea time, and extensive paired endings. It also significantly expanded same-sex options, though initially faced criticism for an imbalance between male and female Byleth's romantic choices, later addressed with DLC. A notable point of contention, however, was the ethical implications of student-teacher relationships, given Byleth's role as a professor.

The most recent title, *Fire Emblem Engage* (FE17), scaled back the paired endings, making them almost entirely avatar-centric through an optional side mission. While support conversations still exist and show romantic chemistry, the lack of traditional paired endings for non-avatar characters felt underdeveloped to some players.
